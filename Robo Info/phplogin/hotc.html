1800

Charles Babbage

Charles Babbage (1791-1871) was an English mathematician and inventor who is credited with designing the first mechanical computer. He was a polymath who made significant contributions to fields like mathematics, engineering, economics, and computer science. Babbage was born in London and studied mathematics at Cambridge University, where he became interested in the idea of a machine that could perform mathematical calculations

Babbage's most famous invention was the Analytical Engine, a machine that could perform a variety of mathematical operations and was designed to be programmed with punch cards. Although the Analytical Engine was never completed during Babbage's lifetime due to lack of funding, it is considered to be a forerunner of modern computers and is an important milestone in the history of computing.

Apart from his work on computers, Babbage was also interested in economics and published several influential works on the subject. He believed that the economy could be analyzed using mathematical methods and that this would lead to more efficient use of resources. Babbage was a member of the Royal Society and received numerous honors for his contributions to science and technology. Today, he is remembered as one of the pioneers of computing and his ideas continue to influence the development of modern computers.

1937-1943

John Atanasoff and Clifford Berry

In 1937, John Atanasoff and Clifford Berry created the first electronic computer, which they called the Atanasoff-Berry Computer (ABC). It used binary digits and could solve linear equations, paving the way for modern computing.

During World War II, computers were used to crack secret codes, leading to the development of the first programmable computer, the Colossus. This machine was built in 1943 and was used by the British to decrypt Nazi messages.

1945-1950

John von Neumann

In 1945, John von Neumann proposed the idea of a stored-program computer, where instructions and data could be stored in the same memory. This was a significant breakthrough and led to the development of the first general-purpose computer, the Electronic Numerical Integrator and Computer (ENIAC).

The 1950s saw the emergence of commercial computers, such as the UNIVAC and IBM 701. These machines were expensive and only affordable to large corporations and government agencies.

1960-Present

Modern Day

In the 1960s and 1970s, computers became smaller, faster, and more affordable, leading to the development of personal computers. The first personal computer, the Altair 8800, was released in 1975 and was followed by the Apple I in 1976 and the Apple II in 1977.

The 1980s saw the introduction of graphical user interfaces (GUIs), making computers more user-friendly. This was followed by the rise of the internet and the World Wide Web in the 1990s, which revolutionized the way we communicate and access information.

Today, computers are an integral part of our daily lives, from smartphones and laptops to supercomputers used for scientific research and data analysis. The history of computers has been marked by innovation and progress, leading to an ever-evolving landscape of technology that continues to shape the world we live i
